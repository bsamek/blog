There's an old business idea, usually attributed to Michael Gerber, about the difference between working *in* your business and working *on* your business. Working in the business is doing the thing. Working on the business is improving how the business works.

I wrote a feature at work recently in a day that previously would have taken more than a week. The difference wasn't that I cut corners. It was that writing code — the part that used to dominate — took a much smaller fraction of the time. The LLM handled most of it. But everything else didn't shrink. Figuring out the right approach. Thinking through edge cases. Writing tests that actually test what matters. Validating the work in staging. Talking through the architecture. That stuff took the same amount of time it always did, and it was suddenly most of the day. The coding was working *in* the feature. Everything around it was working *on* it.

The things that surround the code — test design, validation, making sure the approach is right before writing a line — are going to matter more now, not less. Previously, getting faster at writing code was a good investment because writing code was most of the work. Now that it's cheap, improving those other things is where the leverage is. They're also where the quality comes from, and probably always were.
