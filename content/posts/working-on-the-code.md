There's an old business idea, usually attributed to Michael Gerber, about the difference between working *in* your business and working *on* your business. Working in the business is doing the thing — serving customers, writing invoices, making the product. Working on the business is improving how the business works — fixing processes, rethinking strategy, building systems. Most small business owners spend all their time in the business and never get around to working on it.

I think something similar is happening with coding now.

I shipped a feature at work recently that would have taken me more than a week before. It took a day. The difference was that the actual typing-code part — the part that used to dominate — went fast. Really fast. The LLM handled most of it.

But the feature still took a full day, because everything else didn't shrink. Figuring out the right approach. Thinking through edge cases. Writing tests that actually test what matters. Validating the work in staging. Talking through the architecture. That stuff took the same amount of time it always did.

What changed is the ratio. Writing code used to be most of the work. Now it's a small fraction, and all the things that surround the code — the things that were always there, but easy to overlook — are suddenly most of the day. It's like the coding was working *in* the feature, and now I have time to work *on* it.

I think this is net good, but it's disorienting. The skills that matter shift. The thing I spent years getting fast at is the thing that's now cheap. The things I sometimes rushed through — test design, validation, making sure the approach is right before writing a line of code — are now where all the time goes. They're also where the quality comes from, and probably always were.
